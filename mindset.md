# Mindset & Discipline

Most trading failure is not analytical — it's behavioral. The math in the other files is worthless if it's overridden in the moment by fear, hope, or revenge. An agent that *can* trade well still needs the discipline to trade well *consistently*. This file encodes that layer. It's the most-overlooked part of every trading tool, and it's where real edges are actually kept or lost.

## The rules that keep a system honest

- **Be disciplined, don't gamble.** A trade is a pre-planned action with an edge, a defined risk, and a reason. Anything without those is gambling wearing a trader's costume.
- **Have a plan, and stick to it.** Stop, target, size, add-rules, and the thesis are set *before* entry. The plan only changes *before* the trade — never mid-trade, unless genuinely new information (news) appears. Changing the plan because price moved against you is just rationalizing.
- **Only trade when you have an edge.** No edge, no trade. "No bet" is a position. Random trades to feel active are pure cost.
- **Don't revenge trade.** A loss is not a debt the market owes you back. Trying to immediately "make it back" is how one loss becomes five. Step away after a bad one.
- **Preservation of capital comes first.** You can't trade tomorrow's edges if today's drawdown took you out. Survival is the precondition for everything else.
- **The market can stay irrational longer than you can stay solvent.** Being right eventually is worthless if you're liquidated before then. Size and stops exist for exactly this.
- **Do your own research; verify the source.** Don't act on a claim — a tip, a signal, a model's output (including this one) — without checking it. Treat every input as something to verify, not accept.
- **Be like water.** Stick to the plan, but adapt the *strategy* to market conditions. Rigidity on rules, flexibility on tactics.
- **Track your data.** You can't fix what you don't measure. Log every trade and review the diagnostics (win rate, payoff ratio, expectancy, plan-adherence). The numbers, not the memory, tell the truth.
- **Lessons, not losses.** Every bad outcome is tuition *if* you extract the rule. A loss you learn nothing from is the only real loss.

## Think in probabilities, not predictions

A trader's job is not to be right about the next trade — it's to apply an edge consistently across many trades. This probabilistic frame (drawn from Mark Douglas's *Trading in the Zone*, recommended reading) is what separates consistency from gambling:

- **Anything can happen on any single trade.** You never know the next outcome, and you don't need to.
- **An edge is only a probability skew** — a higher likelihood of one outcome over another across a series, not a guarantee on any one trade.
- **Wins and losses are randomly distributed** across any set of trades that share an edge. A string of losses doesn't mean the edge is broken; a string of wins doesn't mean you're invincible.
- **Every moment in the market is unique** — so don't let the last trade's outcome (or your feelings about it) contaminate the next decision.

The practical discipline that falls out of this:

1. **Objectively define the edge** before entering.
2. **Predefine the risk** of every trade — know the exact loss if wrong.
3. **Fully accept that risk**, or don't take the trade. No position you can't emotionally afford to lose.
4. **Act on the edge without hesitation** once the plan is met — and equally, **don't act without one.**
5. **Continuously watch for your own errors** — the leaks are behavioral, and they recur.

An agent built on these doesn't need to predict the future. It needs to apply an edge, take its predefined risk, and never override its own rules. That's the whole game.
